Mary's Column


The column of Mary was ordered by Antal Grassalkovich I in 1771. The baroque artwork is identical with the work of Martin Vögerl (1714-1770) Austrian sculptor in Hainburg. The multi-figured, free-standing memorial sculpture was erected in the middle of the Market Square (later Church Square, Ferenc József Square, today Szabadság Square). Before the statue, in the 18th century, two fountains were there. The statue was erected as a vow, to protect against the plague epidemic. The landlord wanted to use the Mary's column for open-air public ceremonies.

The column rises out from a volute base, which is surrounded by a rectangular, folded, plaited, pierced, stone railing. From the front view there is an altar at the bottom. The four sides of the lower part of the pedestal are decorated with reliefs: 1. Assumption. 2. Visitation. 3. The Annunciation. 4. Presentation of Mary in the temple. Over the reliefs there are Latin explanatory texts. There are four stone lanterns under the volutes. On the top of the base, on the four corners of the ledge there are the four patron saints; St. Teresa, St. Anthony of Padua, St. Roch, St. Florian. Between the statues of the saints there are also stone lanterns. The flower-decorated column with the figure of Mary emerges from the embellishments.
