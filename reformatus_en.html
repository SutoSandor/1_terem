Protestants in Gödöllő


Gödöllő depopulated during the Fifteen Years' War (1593-1606), it was registered as a wilderness between 1594 and 1649. At the time of the moving back, the landlords also returned along with the serfs. The returning residents, both the old families from Gödöllő and the new families were of the Protestant faith. Their church, the medieval stone church standing on the site of today's castle was renovated and rebuilt in 1657. From this time we know the first preacher of the town, Mihály Némedy. His successor for decades was Mihály Marosi, then his son, Mihály Marosi Jr. The oldest devotional objects of the Gödöllő Protestants are also from the 17th century.

It also brought a significant change in the lives of the Protestants, when the Roman Catholic Antal Grassalkovich I. made Gödöllő the center of his lands in the 18th century. He wanted to build his castle on the hill where the Protestant Church stood. The church was demolished and the cemetery around transported beyond the borders of the village. Grassalkovich, as a compensation, donated land, building materials and money to build a new Protestant church. The church with the help of donations was ultimately finished in September 1744, the bell was put in the tower in 1768. The school was built next to the church.

The landlord settled Catholic serfs next to the Protestant population. The two denominations have been living side by side in Gödöllő since then. In 1775, the landowner's widow, Terézia Klobusitzky, taking advantage of the death of the former preacher, tried to force the Protestants to deny their faith. The case ended with the continuation of the Protestants' freedom of worship.

The Protestant Church still serves the faithful of the parish.
